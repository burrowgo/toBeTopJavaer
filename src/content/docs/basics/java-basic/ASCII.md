---
title: "Ascii"
---

ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange) is a computer encoding system based on the Latin alphabet, primarily used to display modern English and other Western European languages.

It is the most common single-byte encoding system today and is equivalent to the international standard ISO/IEC 646.

Standard ASCII code, also known as Basic ASCII, uses a 7-bit binary number (the remaining 1 bit is 0) to represent all uppercase and lowercase letters, digits 0 to 9, punctuation marks, and special control characters used in American English.

Among them:

0-31 and 127 (33 in total) are control characters or communication-specific characters (the rest are displayable characters), such as control characters: LF (line feed), CR (carriage return), FF (form feed), DEL (delete), BS (backspace), BEL (bell), etc.; communication-specific characters: SOH (start of heading), EOT (end of transmission), ACK (acknowledge), etc.

ASCII values 8, 9, 10, and 13 are converted into backspace, tab, line feed, and carriage return characters, respectively. They do not have specific graphical displays but will affect the text display differently depending on the application.

32-126 (95 in total) are characters (32 is space), where 48-57 are the ten Arabic numerals from 0 to 9.

65-90 are the 26 uppercase English letters, 97-122 are the 26 lowercase English letters, and the rest are punctuation marks, mathematical operators, etc.
